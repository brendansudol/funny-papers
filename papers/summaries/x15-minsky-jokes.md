# Jokes and the Logic of the Cognitive Unconscious

**Marvin Minsky** — MIT AI Memo 603 · Guide entry Theory (also worth knowing) (Theory Foundations)

[paper page](https://dspace.mit.edu/handle/1721.1/6300) · [local PDF](../pdfs/x15-minsky-jokes.pdf) · [full markdown](../md/x15-minsky-jokes/x15-minsky-jokes.md) · [extract](../extracts/x15-minsky-jokes.json)

## TL;DR
Minsky offers a theory of humor as part of the mind’s machinery for learning to avoid bad reasoning. The central claim is that jokes, especially nonsense jokes, expose dangerous or defective patterns of thought so that unconscious “censors” can learn to suppress them; laughter both interrupts the bad line of reasoning and focuses attention on it.

## Problem & Motivation
The essay starts from Freud’s theory that jokes evade unconscious censors and release psychic energy tied to forbidden thoughts. Minsky argues that Freud’s account fits jokes about aggression and sexuality better than it fits humorous nonsense. The paper’s motivation is to make these forms of humor look more similar by treating faulty reasoning as something the mind also needs to prohibit. Common-sense logic is powerful but unreliable: paradoxes, circular reasoning, false analogies, misleading metaphors, and inappropriate self-reference can make thought meander, repeat, or collapse into confusion. Humor, on this view, is connected to how people learn where not to reason further.

## Approach
The paper proposes a theoretical account rather than an experiment. Minsky introduces “cognitive censors,” mental mechanisms that recognize precursors to destructive or ineffective thought processes and suppress them before they develop. He sketches a two-brain model: an A-brain acts on and perceives the world, while a B-brain monitors internal A-states and can recognize metapsychological conditions such as meandering, circling, or confusion.

The theory is linked to Minsky’s frame account of knowledge. Frames represent stereotyped situations with terminals, defaults, and links to related frames. Much reasoning and analogy involves shifting between frames. Humor often arises when a scene is first understood through one frame and then, often through a single word, is suddenly reinterpreted through another. Some shifts are insightful; others are absurd or dangerous. The joke marks the problematic shift for future suppression.

## Data & Experimental Setup
There is no dataset, model, or empirical evaluation. The essay uses examples from Freud and other sources: the cake-for-liqueur joke, the “knife without a blade which has no handle,” liar and Barber paradoxes, the “Life is like a bridge” joke, the dog-hunting joke, Quine’s self-reference example, and Freud’s mayonnaise/spinach joke. These are illustrative cases for a theory of jokes, nonsense, puns, paradoxes, and forbidden subjects.

## Results
The paper reports no quantitative results. Its main theoretical result is an explanatory structure: familiar jokes become less funny because a censor has been built or extended; nonsense is funny because it exposes cognitive bugs; and laughter has two complementary roles. First, “Laughter disrupts reasoning,” preventing the mind from continuing along a ridiculous or prohibited path. Second, “Laughter focusses attention,” holding the absurdity in mind so a censor-learning mechanism can absorb it.

Minsky also argues that the persistence of sexual humor does not necessarily falsify the theory. Instead, it may mean that censors associated with sexual subjects are slower to learn and change than censors for ordinary nonsense jokes.

## Takeaways
- Humor systems should not treat jokes only as surface wordplay; the essay emphasizes failures of inference, analogy, metaphor, and frame selection.
- Reinterpretation is central: many jokes depend on an unexpected frame-substitution that changes how all prior elements are understood.
- Repetition matters because the theory predicts reduced funniness once the listener has learned the censor for that joke type.
- Nonsense humor can be understood as cognitively serious: it marks patterns of thought that should not be trusted.
- Any broad theory of humor must handle overlapping mechanisms, not a single clean essence.

## Limitations & Caveats
This is a speculative theory essay with no experiments, no implementation, and no measurements. Minsky explicitly says his evolutionary scenario is not defended in its particulars. He also warns that “humor” may not name a sharply bounded mental thing; different people may use the word for different web-like models. Adult humor further complicates the account because later cultural and cognitive development can reuse early mechanisms in ways that blur the distinction between encouraging and suppressing frame-shifts.
