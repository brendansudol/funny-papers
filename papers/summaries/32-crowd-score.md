# Crowd Score: A Method for the Evaluation of Jokes using Large Language Model AI Voters as Judges

**Fabrício Góes, Zisen Zhou, Piotr Sawicki, Marek Grzes, Daniel G. Brown** — arXiv:2212.11214 · Guide entry #32 (Part 4 - Evaluation Methodology)

[paper page](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.11214) · [local PDF](../pdfs/32-crowd-score.pdf) · [full markdown](../md/32-crowd-score/32-crowd-score.md) · [extract](../extracts/32-crowd-score.json)

## TL;DR
The paper proposes Crowd Score, a method for evaluating jokes by using GPT-3 as a crowd of AI judges with different humor-style personalities. In a 52-joke case study, the aggregated Crowd Score follows the same general trend as human ratings, and prompt design strongly affects binary humor-classification accuracy.

## Problem & Motivation
Computational creativity work often relies on human judges to evaluate creative artifacts, including jokes, but human evaluation is costly and hard to standardize. The authors argue that recent LLMs can act like non-expert human judges for creative evaluation. They focus on joke funniness because humor is difficult for machines and because the selected joke dataset includes human funniness ratings for comparison.

## Approach
Crowd Score has four steps. First, the authors tune a binary voting question that asks GPT-3 to classify a joke as Funny or an opposite label such as Boring or Not Funny. Second, they induce voter personalities using four humor styles: affiliative, self-enhancing, aggressive and self-defeating. Third, each voter gives a classification plus an explanation generated with a step-by-step prompt, and an auditing prompt asks whether the reasoning explains the classification. Fourth, validated binary votes are summed; with four personalities, the raw score is in the range [0,4], and for comparison to human ratings it is normalized to [1,4].

## Data & Experimental Setup
The study uses the joke dataset from Toplyn [4]. It contains 13 headline-style inputs; for each input, four jokes were generated by OpenAI GPT-3, Witscript, Witscript 2 and a professional human comedian, giving 52 jokes total. The paper states that all jokes are in the aggressive/self-defeating spectrum and rely on common sense rather than wordplay. Experiments use GPT-3 with text-davinci-002, temperature 1 and top P 1. All experiments were run 3 times and results are arithmetic averages. For full-dataset binary evaluation, jokes with human rating greater than or equal to 2 are treated as funny, giving 37 funny and 15 not funny jokes.

## Results
On the 8-joke tuning subset, Funny/Boring and Funny/Dull were best: both achieved F-Score 0.89 and balanced accuracy 0.88 in zero-shot, and F-Score 1 and balanced accuracy 1 in few-shot. The paper reports that choosing the least appropriate opposite word can reduce balanced accuracy by 26% in zero-shot and 25% in few-shot. On all 52 jokes, Funny/Boring achieved F-Score 0.78 and balanced accuracy 0.7 in zero-shot, versus 0.59 and 0.6 for Funny/Not Funny. In few-shot, Funny/Boring reached 0.83 and 0.78, versus 0.81 and 0.76 for Funny/Not Funny. Personality induction behaved as expected: aggressive and self-defeating voters found more jokes funny than affiliative and self-enhancing voters. The audit invalidated 14% of all votes. For the top 10 jokes, only one score in 20 was below 3; for the bottom 10 jokes, only 5 in 20 scores were above 3.

## Takeaways
- LLM-as-judge humor evaluation is feasible in this case study, but prompt wording is not a minor detail.
- For binary joke voting, Boring worked better than the naive Not Funny label, especially in zero-shot.
- Personality prompts can create useful voter diversity, but few-shot examples can overwrite the induced personality.
- Automated auditing is useful but not settled; the current prompt discarded some votes a human expert considered valid.

## Limitations & Caveats
The study uses only 52 jokes, all from an aggressive/self-defeating joke set. The same model audits its own explanations, which the authors flag as a possible source of bias. The voting question, personality descriptions, auditing prompts and aggregation rule are all hand-designed, and the paper leaves automated prompt discovery, stronger auditing and alternative aggregation methods for future work.
