# A Two-Stage Model for the Appreciation of Jokes and Cartoons: An Information-Processing Analysis

## TL;DR
Suls proposes a two-stage, information-processing model of how people appreciate verbal jokes and captioned cartoons. The core claim is that humor requires both a surprising incongruity and a successful resolution: the recipient must find a cognitive rule that makes the punch line or caption follow from the preceding material.

## Problem & Motivation
The chapter aims to explain humor appreciation in cognitive and structural terms while still allowing motivational, emotional, and situational factors to matter. Suls focuses on narrative forms of humor—verbal jokes and captioned cartoons—where a sequence of ideas leads to an ending. He argues that earlier accounts often emphasized surprise or disconfirmation but did not explain why surprise becomes pleasurable, and that Freudian accounts overemphasized motive release as the basis of humor.

## Approach
The proposed model has two stages. In Stage 1, the recipient reads or hears the setup, stores contextual information, forms a narrative schema, and predicts what will come next. If the ending violates that prediction, the recipient experiences surprise or incongruity. In Stage 2, the recipient tries to solve the problem posed by the incongruity: how does the punch line follow from the stem? Suls models this as problem solving similar to the General Problem Solver, where cognitive rules—logical propositions, definitions, or facts of experience—serve as operators that reduce the difference between the setup and ending. If a satisfactory rule is found, the joke is “got” and laughter can result; if not, the recipient remains puzzled.

## Data & Experimental Setup
This is a theoretical chapter, not an empirical study. It does not introduce a dataset, run models, recruit participants, or report a new evaluation. The argument is developed through illustrative jokes, including examples about “Fat Ethel,” a prostitute, and O’Riley on trial for armed robbery. The model is explicitly scoped to verbal jokes and captioned cartoons and excludes nonnarrative humor such as slapstick, exaggerated gestures and facial expressions, captionless cartoons, and political caricatures.

## Results
There are no new quantitative results and no system comparisons. The chapter’s concrete output is the flow-chart model of humor appreciation and a set of predicted factors influencing funniness: incongruity of the joke ending, complexity of Stage-2 problem solving, time taken to solve the incongruity problem, and salience of the joke’s content. Suls cites prior work as support, including Shurcliff’s reported monotonic relationship between surprisingness and funniness and Goldstein’s finding that appreciation decreased as processing time increased, but no exact effect sizes are given in this chapter.

## Takeaways
- Incongruity is necessary but not sufficient; an unresolved surprise should produce puzzlement, not humor.
- A humor system inspired by this account would need to model both expectation violation and a recoverable rule that explains the violation.
- Funniness depends not only on the joke text but also on the recipient’s schemas, accessible knowledge, motivation, and processing ease.
- Very easy resolutions may feel trivial, while very hard ones may fail; Suls predicts appreciation should peak around a moderate level of difficulty.
- Evaluation of humor should distinguish surprise, resolution difficulty, solution time, and content salience rather than treating them as one factor.

## Limitations & Caveats
The chapter is conceptual and calls for future experiments to test and weight the proposed factors. It acknowledges that the model is not final, that the broad claim about resolved incongruity may need qualifications, and that repeated exposure remains a challenge for incongruity theories, though several possible explanations are offered.
