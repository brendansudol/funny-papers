# How funny is ChatGPT? A comparison of human- and A.I.-produced jokes

**Drew Gorenz, Norbert Schwarz** — PLOS One 2024 · Guide entry #31 (Part 4 - Evaluation Methodology)

[paper page](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0305364) · [local PDF](../pdfs/31-how-funny-is-chatgpt.pdf) · [full markdown](../md/31-how-funny-is-chatgpt/31-how-funny-is-chatgpt.md) · [extract](../extracts/31-how-funny-is-chatgpt.json)

## TL;DR
Gorenz and Schwarz compare ChatGPT 3.5 with humans on written humor production. In Study 1, blind lay raters judged ChatGPT 3.5’s jokes funnier than laypeople’s jokes overall (M_ChatGPT = 2.63 vs. M_Humans = 2.20), and 69.5% preferred the A.I. responses. In Study 2, ChatGPT 3.5’s satirical headlines were statistically indistinguishable from The Onion’s professional headlines.

## Problem & Motivation
The paper asks whether an LLM can produce novel humor that ordinary audiences actually find funny. Prior work, according to the authors, mostly offered anecdotal examples of ChatGPT succeeding or failing at humor, without systematic comparison to human joke production. Because comedy depends on audience response, the authors focus on lay ratings rather than expert judgments.

## Approach
The authors ran two preregistered studies. Study 1 compared ChatGPT 3.5 with laypeople on three humor-production tasks: an acronym task, a fill-in-the-blank task, and a roast joke task. Study 2 compared ChatGPT 3.5 with professional comedy writers by asking it to generate satirical headlines in the style of The Onion’s “Local” section. In both studies, raters saw jokes without source labels and rated funniness on a 0–6 scale.

## Data & Experimental Setup
In Study 1, 123 U.S. MTurk workers were recruited through CloudResearch to produce jokes; 18 were excluded for self-reported copying and pasting, leaving N = 105. They produced 945 human comedic responses across 9 prompts. ChatGPT 3.5 received the same tasks and instructions but generated 20 answers per prompt, yielding 180 A.I. responses. A separate N = 200 U.S. MTurk sample rated 54 responses each.

In Study 2, the authors retrieved the last 50 The Onion “Local” headlines before October 1, 2023 and prompted ChatGPT 3.5 with those examples to generate 20 new headlines. They randomly selected 20 of the 50 Onion headlines as the professional human set. N = 217 USC students rated 10 headlines each, 5 from ChatGPT and 5 from The Onion.

## Results
In Study 1, ChatGPT 3.5 beat laypeople overall: M_ChatGPT = 2.63, 95% CI = [2.48, 2.79], versus M_Humans = 2.20, 95% CI = [2.06, 2.34]. It was significantly better on all three tasks: acronym t(199) = 4.92, p < .00001; fill-in-the-blank t(199) = 2.57, p = .0108; roast t(199) = 10.78, p < .00001. Figure 1 reports task means of 2.45 vs. 2.11 for acronyms, 2.44 vs. 2.29 for fill-in-the-blank, and 3.01 vs. 2.20 for roasts, always favoring ChatGPT. ChatGPT performed above 73% of human producers on acronyms, 63% on fill-in-the-blank, and 87% on roasts. Overall, 69.5% of raters preferred A.I. responses, 26.5% preferred human responses, and 4.0% rated them equally funny.

In Study 2, The Onion averaged 2.49, 95% CI = [2.32, 2.66], while ChatGPT averaged 2.37, 95% CI = [2.19, 2.55]; the difference was not significant, t(216) = 1.48, p = .14. The most highly rated headline was generated by ChatGPT.

## Takeaways
- For text-only joke writing under clear prompts, ChatGPT 3.5 can outperform average laypeople.
- Strong prompt context and examples mattered: the A.I. saw task constraints and, in Study 2, 50 Onion examples.
- Blind human evaluation is central here; the results concern audience-rated funniness, not expert assessment.
- Builders of humor systems should test against human baselines and professional references, not only inspect examples.
- The roast result suggests ChatGPT could produce aggressive humor judged funny despite safety-oriented constraints.

## Limitations & Caveats
The work evaluates only ChatGPT 3.5, only written text, and only joke quality rather than output quantity. It does not test delivery, images, video, memes, stand-up, or source-attribution effects. Study 2 used a USC student convenience sample and timeless Onion “Local” headlines because ChatGPT 3.5 lacked current-news updates. The authors also caution that generic or ambiguous prompts may produce worse humor than the structured prompts used here.
