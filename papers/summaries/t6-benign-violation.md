# Benign Violations: Making Immoral Behavior Funny

**A. Peter McGraw, Caleb Warren** — Psychological Science · Guide entry T6 (Theory Foundations)

[paper page](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0956797610376073) · [local PDF](../pdfs/t6-benign-violation.pdf) · [full markdown](../md/t6-benign-violation/t6-benign-violation.md) · [extract](../extracts/t6-benign-violation.json)

## TL;DR
McGraw and Warren propose and test the benign-violation hypothesis: humor occurs when something is simultaneously appraised as a violation and as benign. In five text-scenario experiments on moral violations, violation versions elicited more laughter or amusement than controls, with Study 1 showing 44% laughter for violations versus 5% for controls.

## Problem & Motivation
The paper starts from a tension between humor theory and moral psychology. Humor theories often say funny things involve violations of expectations, norms, dignity, or safety, while moral psychology typically treats moral violations as elicitors of negative emotions, especially disgust. The authors argue that these views can be reconciled if violations are funny only when they also seem acceptable, safe, distant, or otherwise benign.

## Approach
The proposed account has three jointly necessary and sufficient conditions: a situation must be appraised as a violation, appraised as benign, and both appraisals must occur simultaneously. The paper tests three routes by which a moral violation can become benign: another salient norm suggests the behavior is acceptable, the observer is weakly committed to the violated norm, or the violation is psychologically distant.

## Data & Experimental Setup
All studies used short written scenarios and human responses. Study 1 had 66 campus participants read violation and control versions of four scenarios and report whether the behavior was wrong and whether it made them laugh. Study 2 had 73 participants read the Snorting Remains violation or control; a blind research assistant coded smiles/laughter, and participants judged whether the behavior could be seen as wrong, not wrong, or both. Study 3 used a kitten scenario with harmless versus harmful versions (N = 36). Study 4 manipulated a church versus credit-union Hummer raffle and used church attendance as a commitment proxy (N = 80). Study 5 primed far versus near psychological distance before a chicken violation or control scenario (73 undergraduate participants; reported tests use N = 72 or N = 40 depending on comparison).

## Results
Study 1 found that violation versions were judged wrong more often than controls (69% vs. 2%; F(1, 58) = 576.5, p < .001) and produced more laughter (44% vs. 5%; F(1, 58) = 112.4, p < .001). Participants both judged the violation wrong and laughed 27% of the time versus 0% for controls. In Study 2, the violation version produced more observed amusement than the control (32% vs. 8%; χ²(1, N = 73) = 6.5, p = .01), and people who interpreted the behavior as both wrong and not wrong were more amused than those who did not (44% vs. 13%; χ²(1, N = 73) = 8.5, p < .01). Study 3 showed the harmless kitten violation was more amusing than the harmful version (61% vs. 28%) and produced more amusement-disgust (56% vs. 22%). Study 4 showed low-commitment nonchurchgoers were more amused than churchgoers by the church-Hummer violation (92% vs. 62%) and more often both amused and disgusted (69% vs. 35%). Study 5 showed far psychological distance increased amusement toward the chicken violation (73% vs. 39%) and mixed amusement-disgust (64% vs. 28%).

## Takeaways
- Humor systems or evaluations should not treat violation alone as sufficient for funniness; benignness and simultaneity matter.
- Negative affect can coexist with amusement, especially for taboo or moral humor.
- Distance, weak norm commitment, and alternative acceptable interpretations are concrete levers that change whether a violation is funny.
- Audience norms matter: the same scenario can be funny to one group and mainly disgusting to another.

## Limitations & Caveats
The experiments are limited to moral-violation scenarios, mostly with campus or undergraduate participants. Other domains such as puns, slapstick, teasing, and tickling are discussed but not experimentally tested. The authors also note no consistent gender effects and flag a possible Study 4 confound: “Hummer” may have unintentionally created a sexual double meaning in the control condition.
