# Script theory revis(it)ed: joke similarity and joke representation model

**Salvatore Attardo, Victor Raskin** — HUMOR 4:293-347; expanded in Attardo, Linguistic Theories of Humor (1994) · Guide entry T2 (Theory Foundations)

[De Gruyter article page (consulted)](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/humr.1991.4.3-4.293/html?lang=en) · [extract](../extracts/t2-gtvh.json)

## TL;DR
Attardo and Raskin propose the General Theory of Verbal Humor (GTVH), a theory of verbal jokes built around six knowledge resources: script opposition, logical mechanism, situation, target, narrative strategy, and language. The main result is a hierarchical joke-representation model that revises SSTH and Attardo’s earlier five-level model, plus a taxonomy of joke variants and invariants indexed by shared knowledge-resource values.

## Problem & Motivation
The paper asks how verbal jokes can be represented so that similarities and differences among jokes are captured systematically. It begins from the observation that many jokes are retold as paraphrases or variants: some versions differ only in wording, while others differ in target, situation, punchline logic, or underlying script opposition. Existing accounts are treated as insufficient: Raskin’s SSTH focuses on script opposition, and Attardo’s earlier five-level model contains mixed or awkward levels. The authors aim to build a more general theory of verbal humor, focusing on verbal jokes as the representative case.

## Approach
The paper first compares seven light-bulb-style joke examples, each differing from joke (1) along one dimension. From these differences it identifies six parameters, later called knowledge resources: Language (LA), Narrative Strategy (NS), Target (TA), Situation (SI), Logical Mechanism (LM), and Script Opposition (SO). It then argues for a hierarchy using three sources: asymmetrical binary relations among the KRs, a modified content/tool distinction, and hypothesized perceptions of joke similarity. The final hierarchy is: Script Oppositions → Logical Mechanisms → Situations → Target → Narrative Strategies → Language → Joke Text.

## Data & Experimental Setup
There is no empirical experiment, dataset, model, or participant sample. The evidence consists of theoretical analysis of joke examples, including examples from cited joke collections and constructed examples used to isolate parameters. The authors explicitly say the similarity judgments are based on weak psychological intuitions and could be tested or falsified by psychological experiments. The theory is not a production model: it does not claim that speakers temporally create jokes by moving through the hierarchy.

## Results
The central output is a six-resource theory replacing the earlier five-level representation. The six-argument joke template is {SO, LM, SI, TA, NS, LA}. Variants are indexed by which arguments they share: for example, jokes (1) and (2) are {SO, LM, SI, TA, NS} variants because they differ only in language, while jokes (1) and (6) are {SO, SI, TA, NS, LA} variants because they differ in logical mechanism. Target is the only optional KR; some jokes lack a clear target. No system beats another, and no quantitative accuracy or statistical comparison is reported.

## Takeaways
- Humor representation can be decomposed into separable resources rather than treated only as script opposition.
- For system builders, GTVH suggests that joke generation or analysis should control not just topic and wording, but also logical mechanism, situation, target, and narrative strategy.
- Similarity between jokes can be described by shared KR values, enabling a structured notion of paraphrase, variant, and invariant.
- The theory distinguishes analytical representation from actual production, so it should not be read as a cognitive timeline for joke creation.
- Cultural stereotypes are central to targeted jokes, making target selection both theoretically important and ethically sensitive.

## Limitations & Caveats
The paper is theoretical and does not validate the hierarchy with participants or statistical tests. Its strongest claims are about verbal jokes, not all forms of humor. The authors also state that GTVH answers the essentialist “what is humor?” question, not questions about humor’s social functions, origins, or pleasures. Because many examples involve ethnic stereotypes, applying the framework requires care about harmful targets and cultural assumptions.
