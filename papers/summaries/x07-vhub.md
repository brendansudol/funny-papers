# v-HUB: A Benchmark for Video Humor Understanding from Vision and Sound

**Zhengpeng Shi, Yanpeng Zhao, Jianqun Zhou, Yuxuan Wang, Qinrong Cui, Wei Bi, Songchun Zhu, Bo Zhao, Zilong Zheng** — arXiv:2509.25773 · Guide entry Part 3 (video humor) (Part 3 - Multimodal & Visual Humor)

[paper page](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25773) · [local PDF](../pdfs/x07-vhub.pdf) · [full markdown](../md/x07-vhub/x07-vhub.md) · [extract](../extracts/x07-vhub.json) · [dataset: v-HUB](../../data/v-hub/)

## TL;DR
v-HUB is a visual-centric video humor benchmark for testing MLLMs on short, mostly non-verbal humorous videos using vision and environmental sound. The main result is that models understand humor much better from human-written text descriptions than from raw video, while audio helps OmniLLMs but does not close the gap.

## Problem & Motivation
Prior humor benchmarks often emphasize linguistic cues such as captions, dialogue, or text descriptions. This paper targets a different setting: humor that humans can appreciate mainly from visual cues, as in silent comedy and short funny videos. The authors argue that this is a stronger test of video MLLMs because the models must identify actions, temporal correspondences, visual text, sound effects, and cultural context without relying on spoken language.

## Approach
The authors introduce v-HUB, a benchmark with three tasks. Caption Matching asks a model to choose the caption that best corresponds to the humor in a video, using one creative caption and four descriptive-caption distractors. Humor Explanation asks the model to explain why a video is funny in 2-3 sentences. Open-ended QA asks temporal, descriptive, and causal questions generated by GPT-4o and then manually verified. They also define three input settings: Text-Only, using human-written descriptions; Video-Only, using raw video frames without audio; and Video+Audio, using visual frames plus environmental sound.

## Data & Experimental Setup
The final dataset contains 1218 videos totaling 4.7h, with an average duration around 14s. It includes 267 Charlie Chaplin silent-film clips and 951 user-generated short funny videos. The paper reports 722 Visual videos, 137 Visual+Text videos, 256 Visual+Audio videos, and 94 Visual+Audio+Text videos. The Open-ended QA set has 81 temporal, 742 descriptive, and 395 causal QA pairs. Eight annotators completed three rounds of annotation, covering humor level, captions, video description, humor-source labels, background knowledge, and humor explanations. Evaluated models include Gemini-2.5-Flash, GPT-4o, Qwen2.5-VL, Intern3.5-VL, Video-SALMONN-2, MiniCPM2.6-o, Qwen2.5-Omni, Qwen2.5-72B, and Qwen2.5-7B.

## Results
Text descriptions dominate raw video. For Qwen2.5-VL, Caption Matching accuracy drops from 0.726 in Text-Only to 0.666 in Video-Only, and Open-ended QA SentBERT drops from 0.760 to 0.445. GPT-4o achieves the best Text-Only Caption Matching accuracy at 0.762. Audio helps several OmniLLMs: MiniCPM2.6-o improves on Caption Matching from 0.362 to 0.442, and Qwen2.5-Omni improves from 0.553 to 0.617. Visual text is useful: for Gemini-2.5-Flash when sound does not contribute to humor, Matching Accuracy rises from 0.569 without visual text to 0.715 with visual text. Background knowledge also helps: Qwen2.5-Omni’s Video+Audio humor explanation SentBERT improves from 0.459 to 0.512, and AutoDQ from 0.127 to 0.176.

## Takeaways
- Current MLLMs struggle to discover humor directly from visual context.
- Human-written descriptions remain a much easier input than raw video.
- Environmental sound is informative, especially for OmniLLMs, but audio fusion is still limited.
- Visual text and explicit background knowledge are strong cues for humor understanding.
- Builders of humor systems should evaluate humor discovery separately from answering guided questions.

## Limitations & Caveats
The authors use region-level cultural categories because single culture labels are ambiguous for globally shared visual humor. All annotations are in English, so results may not capture cross-language variation in how humor is interpreted or expressed. Human evaluation covers 50 explanations scored by one annotator.
