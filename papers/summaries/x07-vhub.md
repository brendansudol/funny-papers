# v-HUB: A Benchmark for Video Humor Understanding from Vision and Sound

**Zhengpeng Shi, Yanpeng Zhao, Jianqun Zhou, Yuxuan Wang, Qinrong Cui, Wei Bi, Songchun Zhu, Bo Zhao, Zilong Zheng** — ACL 2026 (main, long paper) · Guide entry Part 3 (video humor) (Part 3 - Multimodal & Visual Humor)

[paper page](https://aclanthology.org/2026.acl-long.1785/) · [local PDF](../pdfs/x07-vhub.pdf) · [full markdown](../md/x07-vhub/x07-vhub.md) · [extract](../extracts/x07-vhub.json) · [dataset: v-HUB](../../data/v-hub/)

## TL;DR
v-HUB is a visual-centric video humor benchmark built to test whether MLLMs can understand non-verbal humor from video and environmental sound. The central result is that models do much better when given human-written text descriptions than raw video: for example, Qwen2.5-VL drops from 0.726 to 0.666 accuracy on Caption Matching and from 0.760 to 0.445 SentBERT on Open-ended QA.

## Problem & Motivation
Existing humor benchmarks often focus on text, image-text humor, or video humor dominated by speech and linguistic cues. The authors argue that humans can understand many jokes from visuals alone, as in silent comedy, and that current MLLMs need to be tested on this kind of visual-centric humor. They also target environmental sound, such as music and sound effects, which may enhance humor but has been under-examined in video humor evaluation.

## Approach
The paper introduces v-HUB, with three evaluation tasks. Caption Matching asks a model to pick the caption that best corresponds to the humor in a video, using one creative caption and four descriptive-caption distractors. Humor Explanation asks the model to identify humorous points and explain them using visual or auditory cues. Open-ended QA tests more basic video understanding through temporal, descriptive, and causal questions generated by GPT-4o and manually verified.

## Data & Experimental Setup
v-HUB contains 1218 videos: 267 Charlie Chaplin silent humor clips and 951 user-generated funny videos, totaling 4.7h with an average duration around 14s. The authors collected 729 Chaplin clips, 18080 short videos from X @humansnocontext, and 1769 surprising YouTube videos from Xie et al. (2024), then filtered for duration, appropriateness, and speech reliance. Eight annotators produced humor ratings, captions, descriptions, humor-source labels, background knowledge, and explanations through three rounds; more than 90% of data had ‘High’ consensus and 0.3% had ‘Low’ consensus. Evaluated systems include Gemini-2.5-Flash, GPT-4o, Qwen2.5-VL, Intern3.5-VL, Video-SALMONN-2, MiniCPM2.6-o, Qwen2.5-Omni, and Qwen base LLMs. Settings are Text-Only, Video-Only, and Video+Audio.

## Results
Text descriptions dominate raw video. In Caption Matching, the best Text-Only score is GPT-4o at 0.762, while the best Video-Only score is Qwen2.5-VL at 0.666 and the best Video+Audio score is Qwen2.5-Omni at 0.617. Qwen2.5-VL falls from 0.726 Text-Only accuracy to 0.666 Video-Only accuracy; on Open-ended QA, its SentBERT falls from 0.760 to 0.445. Audio helps some OmniLLMs: MiniCPM2.6-o rises from 0.362 to 0.442 Caption Matching accuracy with Video+Audio, and Qwen2.5-Omni rises from 0.553 to 0.617. Background knowledge also helps: Qwen2.5-Omni’s humor explanation improves from SentBERT 0.459 and AutoDQ 0.127 without background knowledge to 0.512 and 0.176 with it.

## Takeaways
- Do not assume video-capable MLLMs can discover humor from visual cues; they still rely strongly on text.
- Humor explanation is harder than answering targeted questions because it requires finding the joke without being pointed to it.
- Environmental sound is useful, but current audio-video fusion remains weak.
- Visual text and explicit background knowledge are strong aids and should be tracked separately in humor benchmarks.
- Historical and cultural context matters: models do worse on Charlie Chaplin clips than contemporary user-generated videos.

## Limitations & Caveats
The cultural analysis uses region-level labels because single culture labels are ambiguous for global visual humor. All annotations are in English, so the benchmark does not test how language-specific descriptions affect humor understanding.
