We also examined whether the discussion stream itself occasionally fails as a conditioning signal: (1) Off topic discussion. For instance, it drifts into high level sociopolitical or philosophical debate. For example, in an “Overbooked Calendars” thread, the discussion quickly reframes the prompt as Brechtian social indictment and moralized commentary (“leisure feels like moral failure . . . laughter . . . is indictment”), then escalates toward capital critique (“Capitalism’s Funniest Home Videos”) and the thread continues along this axis by framing the situation as “maximizing someone else’s profits.” This serves as a poor conditioning signal for subsequent monologue generation. (2) Vacuous Feedback. Instances of weak informational exchange (e.g., "sometimes that’s all you need. . . Would recommend!") that fail to provide substantive direction. As a lightweight sanity check, we manually screened a random sample of 100 discussion messages using our definitions, and observed 1 instance of off-topic drift and 3 instances of low-information feedback. To complement this, we conducted an additional prevalence analysis over all post-performance broadcast discussion messages using an LLM-assisted round-level audit. For each round, we provided GPT-5.1 with the round topic and the full chronological discussion transcript, with each message associated with a unique message ID and speaker role, and asked it to label messages as off-topic drift or low-information feedback when applicable. Using temperature 0, we found that off-topic drift accounted for 55 of 5,384 messages (1.02%), while low-information feedback accounted for 98 of 5,384 messages (1.82%). On the initial 100-message sample, the LLM labels fully agreed with our manual annotations. These results suggest that such fallacy behaviors occur at low rates, which limits their influence on subsequent stand-up monologue generation.
